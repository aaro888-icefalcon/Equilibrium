# TIMELINE NARRATIVE

The thing to understand about what happened is that most of it happened in the first hundred and eighty seconds.

## The Hundred-Eighty Seconds

Every clock stopped at 14:23 UTC on a Thursday afternoon in late May. Every car engine that was running. Every subway that was moving. Every aircraft in the air. Every ventilator in every hospital. Every refrigeration unit, every traffic signal, every cellphone. People on the sidewalks stopped walking and clutched at their heads. A woman in Manhattan near Grand Central lifted a garbage truck off a pedestrian with her hands. A man in Flushing felt his palms turn silver. A teenager in Newark looked at the sky and had the first thought he had ever had that was not his own. The wave of it lasted about three minutes. In those three minutes, forty-five thousand people died in crashes alone.

In the first hour, something in Lower Manhattan stopped being a thing that people could describe, and started being something that ate memory. Nobody knew yet. In the first three hours, twenty-five thousand more died in Manhattan streets from uncontrolled manifestation accidents — kinetic releases that blew out walls, matter-shifts that fused pedestrians into pavement, temporal hiccups where a person stepped off a curb and arrived sixteen feet further along and twelve seconds later, fatally bisected. By sunset the city smelled of blood and ozone and the thing that lives between electrical capacitors when they discharge without warning.

In the first twenty-four hours, a million people died in the mid-Atlantic. In the first week, three million. In the first month, eight million. In the first year, thirty-four million. Of fifty million who had lived there on Wednesday, sixteen million lived there on the following May. That was the baseline.

## The Scavenging Year

The first weeks were water. Then food. Then violence.

Water failed in Manhattan by day three. Pumps require power. People who had not understood that yet understood it then. By day six the first cholera cases began in Queens. By the third week the epidemic peaked — two and a half percent of the remaining NYC metro population died in that single week, most from the simple fact that no one was treating diarrheal illness any more. What saved as many as survived was biokinetics. A group of forty newly-manifested healers in Philadelphia set up at the Inner Harbor Church and took everyone they could. That church became the Bourse's eventual medical wing.

In Newark, a longshoreman named Antonín Volk consolidated the port by day five. He killed about three hundred people in his first week and built the Iron Crown. In the Catskills, a retired Special Forces colonel named James Preston manifested at T6 on Onset Day and went to T9 inside eight weeks through three crisis breakthroughs, each one taking something from him — fear first, warmth second, the ability to speak casually third. In Peekskill, a structural engineer named Dmitri Kostas arrived at Indian Point on day ten with a small team and spent forty continuous hours keeping the reactor contained. His left face and arm turned to stone. The reactor held. It is still holding.

In the Pine Barrens, seven people fled from Camden in week three and wound up at Batsto. They met something there. Five of them stayed.

By the end of month three, the factions were sketched. Preston had the Catskills. Kostas had Peekskill. Volk had Newark. A woman named Maya Okafor at Rutgers was quietly coordinating what would become the Central Jersey League. In Philadelphia the merchants had begun meeting in the old 30th Street Station and calling themselves the Bourse. Down the peninsula, biokinetic farmers had begun making crops larger than they had ever been, and Delmarva had its first rich harvest in October. In DC, General Sarah Alcott had ten flag officers around her and a fiction of government she was willing to maintain.

The first winter killed another twelve percent of who was left. Mostly it was the old, the pharma-dependent, the refugees who had not yet found a community to absorb them. By March of Year One, population had stabilized at about seventeen or eighteen million. The slow decline began.

## The Consolidation

The middle of Year One was economics. Biokinetic agricultural techniques began spreading through the practitioner population following the Rogers diffusion curve; by month nine the inflection point had passed and yields were improving faster than starvation was progressing. In month eight the Bourse issued its first stamped copper coins — fifty thousand of them, in three denominations, backed by scavenged bank-vault reserves. By six weeks later the copper was accepted across the metro. By game start, it was on its way to being the regional standard.

The end of Year One saw the Species I question begin to form. Elder Josefina Flores of PG County had convened the first mid-Atlantic kin assembly on day fifty-two. By month ten she was eighty years old and had the moral weight to preside over four quarterly assemblies. A younger man named Tomás Reyes in Wilmington wanted the Species I diaspora to declare itself a polity. The debate was not resolved. The clock on that question started moving.

The end of Year One also saw the Listening begin to speak. Month twelve, a transformed human messenger appeared simultaneously at the doors of the Philadelphia Bourse, the Central Jersey League Accord Hall, and the Iron Crown's port compound. The messenger delivered a single phrase in each location, declaring that the central Pine Barrens were the Listening's territory and that incursion would be punished. The fact that the messenger appeared at three locations simultaneously was, strictly speaking, impossible. Nobody could explain how.

That is where the game begins.

## What Happens Next

The regional timeline past game-start is not determined. Simulation outputs give probabilities rather than certainties. What follows are the trajectories with the highest probability mass.

The Bourse will complete its currency stability in the first year or two of game time. Copper becomes formal, scrip collapses against it in stages. Contracts begin to be drawn in copper only. Fed Continuity's fiction of sovereignty, which had included the fiction of its currency, becomes noticeably thinner. Alcott will know this. Alcott will understand that the copper system is the end of what she is trying to preserve and she will sign the scrip orders anyway, because the alternative is Reeve.

Somewhere in the first three years past game-start, Iron Crown and Staten will go to open war over Manhattan salvage. The probability is that Staten wins; Chen's spatial-teleportation is harder to counter than Volk's kinetic is to defend. What is less certain is whether Volk survives it. If he dies, Iron Crown fragments into captain-fiefs. Brick Stepanski in Jersey City has been quietly courted by Tower Lords and knows this already.

Somewhere in the same window, Crabclaw and Fed Continuity go to war along the Chesapeake line. Fed Continuity does not win, because it cannot. Alcott will likely cede Baltimore formally. The Erosion clock moves forward.

The first post-Onset-born children begin manifesting around T+6 to T+8. The earliest cohort is small. They manifest at 99% rates, higher than the Onset generation, and their parents study them with particular care. What those children become will define the second post-Onset decade.

Between T+8 and T+11, Fed Continuity erodes past the point Reeve can afford to wait. What happens then is either an open coup or a managed transition, depending on whether Alcott has been able to build a functional counter-faction. Either way, the institution stops pretending to be the federal government in anything but name, and what remains is a DC enclave with ceremonial legitimacy.

Tomás Reyes, sometime between T+15 and T+20, wins the majority of kin-polling and declares Species I a formal polity. Elder Flores by that point is either dead or retired; her coalition has softened as the generational shift has completed. The territorial claim — western Philadelphia, PG County, Wilmington corridor — redraws the regional political map. Baseline-human factions respond variably; the Bourse recognizes formally, the Central Jersey League accepts, the Iron Crown (if still extant) rejects, and Reeve's Irregulars treat it as a threat. New alliances form.

Preston is most likely to die between T+25 and T+30. He is the most formidable individual in the mid-Atlantic and he will be in his seventies. Whether the Catskill Throne survives him as an institution or fragments into retinue-claimants is the single largest uncertainty in the long-run projection. Marshal Whelan is the most obvious successor but does not have Preston's tier. Preston himself has not named a successor and may never.

The Pine Barrens expand slowly. The Listening grows by fifteen to twenty-five percent each year. The Sprawl creeps outward by a few percent. Neither is a crisis in any year. Both compound. Somewhere in the second post-Onset decade, the cult breaches the Delaware line into populated southern New Jersey. Central Jersey League responds as best it can. The response is inadequate. The eldritch frontier has always been patient.

## The Hypothesis of the Trajectory

The canonical simulation projects mid-Atlantic population at 12.42 million at T+30. That is down from 15.94 million at game start — a net decline of 3.5 million across three decades. It is not a collapse. It is also not a recovery. It is what happens when regional attrition from violence, eldritch encroachment, disease, and difficult winters slightly outpaces fertility across a generation.

Five regional great powers at T+30: the Catskill Throne (likely continuing institution with or without Preston); the Central Jersey League (expanded through absorption of peripheral townships); the Species I Polity (formalized for a decade plus, holding western Philadelphia and PG County); the Delmarva Harvest Lords (unified or not; the question may not resolve; either way the region's granary); and the Fed Continuity remnant (reduced to DC Mall and Pentagon enclave, diplomatically recognized as an authority, practically a shadow of Year One's institution).

Between those great powers: a Bourse that has professionalized and extended; a Reeve regime in Virginia that occupies what was Fed Continuity's southern reach; various Tower Lord successor states in the outer boroughs; Species H enclaves intact; Species C and G communities in their niches; the Lehigh Principalities maintaining their coal production; Crabclaw holding Baltimore.

Beyond those: the Pine Barrens, which have expanded; Manhattan, which has contracted; the eldritch frontier, which is permanent now.

The post-Onset-born have inherited. They do not remember the Before; they do not mourn what was lost; they regard manifestation as a birthright and faction loyalty as a choice and the Onset as the thing that happened once and will not happen again. What the Onset generation regarded as catastrophe, they regard as the world. By T+30 they are the majority of the young adult cohort, and they are the ones running things below the most senior level. The Onset generation, those still alive and still working, are grandparents. Their grandchildren have no particular interest in what fell.

What the next thirty years holds is the same shape over again: slow decline within stable bounds, institutional fragility at the top of each faction where indispensable high-tier individuals age and die, the eldritch's patient accumulation. The mid-Atlantic at T+60 will be smaller than at T+30. Whether anything about the pattern breaks — whether a second Onset event, whether a T10+ breakthrough, whether an anomalous surface of something larger — is outside the simulation's projection horizon.

The Lordly Equilibrium is neither utopia nor dystopia. It is a world that absorbed its catastrophe and settled into living afterwards. The characters who live in it marry, have children, bury their dead, trade goods, pay tribute, argue politics. Some of them have powers. A few meet something in the Pine Barrens that changes them. Most do not. The rivers flow the same way they always did. The trees of the Catskills are older than anyone alive. The reactor at Peekskill hums in a register that children born near it feel in their bones before they feel anything else. The fields of Delmarva are greener than they were. The Bourse is open in the morning and closed at dusk. The slow decline continues. The world carries on.
