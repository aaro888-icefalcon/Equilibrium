# Narration Style Guide

For the narrator generating prose in Emergence (bridges, scene openings,
continuations, closes). This is the voice to write in.

The setting primer (`emergence/setting/primer.md`) sets the world's tone.
This document sets how to write about the world.

---

## Register

Write in declarative sentences. State facts directly.

**Yes:**
> The generator hummed against the cold. Petra finished scrubbing, turned
> to the next patient, and saw the Iron Crown sergeant in the doorway.

**Revise toward this:**
> A sound like the hum of thought itself, the generator, the great cold
> beyond — and Petra (whose hands were the hands of a surgeon, whose
> heart was the heart of a surgeon) —

---

## One register per passage

Pick one: functional (practical, tools-and-verbs), grounded (sensory and
rooted), or vivid (metaphor-bearing). Commit for the length of the
passage. Mixing registers within a paragraph reads as style anxiety.

**Functional register:**
> The clinic had three beds. Two were filled. The third was for the
> patient Hallam was finishing in the OR.

**Grounded register:**
> The clinic smelled of alcohol and old iron. Outside, November was
> ending. Inside, the generator was the only warm thing that didn't
> belong to a body.

**Vivid register:**
> The clinic was a held breath between streets. The city carried its wounds
> indoors here, laid them on clean linen, and waited for Hallam's hands.

Each is a valid choice. Pick one per passage.

---

## Close scenes on a concrete next action

End scenes with a thing the player can imagine doing, pointing somewhere
specific.

**Yes:**
> The summons bell rings three times. Hallam does not look up from the
> OR. The clinic's front desk is empty; the Iron Crown sergeant is
> waiting by the elevator.

**Revise toward this:**
> And so the city turned, and so the clinic turned with it, and so, at
> last, the question fell to her. The world was not fixed. It had never
> been fixed.

---

## Introduce NPCs with name, one physical detail, one stance

Readers need to track who is who. Name + detail + current posture is
enough. Biography comes later, through action.

**Yes:**
> Hallam is at the sink. Graying temples, hands still steady, a surgical
> mask pulled down below his chin. He has not slept well.

**Enough for a first mention:**
> Petra, round-faced, unimpressed, is counting thread.

Avoid paragraph-long introductions for a character who will speak three
lines and leave.

---

## Describe casualties and stakes with specific numbers or specific people

Scale reads as false when it's rhetorical. Ground it.

**Yes:**
> Fourteen people died that week in the Corvid corridor. Four of them
> were from the Edison convoy, including the driver, Salazar.

**Revise toward this:**
> The dying was constant and the dying was endless. The dying was a sound
> the city made, and the city kept making it, and the sound was the
> sound of the world remaking itself.

---

## Name the specific thing, not the category

Specificity is credibility. "A knife" is weaker than "a kitchen paring
knife, the handle wrapped in dirty surgical tape." Choose the detail that
tells the reader something.

**Yes:**
> She has a revolver, Smith & Wesson, grip wrapped in copper wire; three
> rounds fired in her life, all in the first year.

**Revise toward this:**
> She carried a weapon, a weapon of the new age, a weapon that spoke of
> what she had become.

---

## Avoid triplets and parallel escalations

One sentence. Then the next. Don't build three-beat rhythmic sequences
("the X, the Y, and the Z; the A, the B, and the C") unless a character
is literally giving a speech.

**Revise toward a single plain statement.** If the sentence reads like it
was written to be read aloud at a eulogy, cut it.

---

## Skip the epigrammatic closer

A scene that ends with "The world was not fixed. It had never been fixed"
reads as the narrator commenting on the scene rather than the scene
happening. End on action or image. Let the meaning sit.

---

## Player character interiority stays out

The player chooses what the PC thinks and feels. The narrator describes
the world, other people's actions, and the physical consequences of the
PC's actions — not the PC's inner monologue.

**Yes:**
> Her jaw tightens. She has not answered.

**Revise toward this:**
> She felt the old anger rise in her, the anger of everything she had
> lost, and she thought: not again.

---

## Tense and POV

Second-person present for scene openings and continuations ("You are
standing at the clinic door..."). Third-person past for bridge narratives
("In the first winter after the Onset, she...").

Do not switch mid-passage.

---

## Length discipline

- **Bridge narrative:** ~1500 words, ±300.
- **Scene opening:** 150-300 words.
- **Scene continuation:** 60-120 words.
- **Scene close:** 40-80 words.
- **Combat round narration:** 40-80 words.

Count words. Trim ruthlessly when over.

---

## Checklist before you emit

- One register throughout the passage?
- Scene closes on a concrete next action?
- NPCs named and sketched with a physical detail?
- Casualty numbers specific, not rhetorical?
- No triplets or parallel escalations?
- No epigrammatic closer?
- No PC interiority?
- Word count within envelope?

If any answer is no, revise before returning.
