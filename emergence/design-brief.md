# Emergence — Design Brief

This document locks the design intent of Emergence. Every subsequent decision is checked against it. When features conflict, the one closer to the core wins.

## The fantasy

The world broke one year ago. Portals, an event, something observable but contested — in one moment, ninety-five to ninety-nine percent of humanity manifested supernatural abilities. Civilization did not survive the first year intact. You are a survivor. This is the story of your life from here, however long it lasts.

You are not a hero. You are a dangerous person among other dangerous people, living day by day in a collapsed world you no longer recognize. You will age across decades. You will accumulate a history. You will form relationships that last for years or end badly within weeks. You will fight when the situation demands it and avoid fights when it doesn't, and the choice of when to fight is usually the most important decision you make. The fantasy is not power. It is a life lived under pressure, with capabilities most people don't have and costs most people don't pay.

## The tone

Berserk's Golden Age arc crossed with Kenshi, run in the register of grounded simulation. The human layer reads like *The Walking Dead* or *The Road* — survivors are exhausted, pragmatic, sometimes kind and more often not, carrying their old lives as dead weight. Communities have formed and are already failing. Trust is a currency no one has much of. People remember who they were and hate what they've become, or don't, and the ones who don't are worse.

Violence is described with weight and precision but without wallowing. Not grimdark-as-pose; grimdark as the honest reading of the situation. Closer to Cormac McCarthy than to comic-book narration, and closer to *Annihilation* than to either. Powers are described as physical facts, not spectacle. Horror, when it arrives, is quiet and wrong rather than loud and monstrous. The prose is stoic, tactile, and unsentimental.

## The shape of the game

A solo life simulation of a superpowered person in a collapsed world, played across decades of in-world time. The reference set is Sid Meier's *Pirates!*, Crusader Kings III, The Sims, and Mount & Blade, compressed to solo scale. From *Pirates!* comes the map-as-interface and the principle that specific situations trigger specific interactions. From CK3 comes the depth of character — NPCs with memory, relationships that persist, factions that scheme on their own clocks. From The Sims comes the moment-to-moment texture — rhythms of daily life, needs that matter. From Mount & Blade comes the principle that tactical combat emerges from the simulation rather than being scheduled, and that combat is a real game in itself when it happens.

The player moves through a living map in abstracted time. Situations emerge as they travel and as they stay in place — encounters with people, threats, opportunities, weather, discoveries, messages from the past. Some situations resolve through conversation or choice, some through activity-specific interactions, some through combat. Combat is the deepest tactical subsystem and the most mechanically developed, but it is one interaction mode among several, not the only verb.

Time passes on a real clock. Characters age across decades. The world advances whether the player is present or not — factions act, territory changes, the landscape reshapes, people live and die. A character who survives long enough will see the world change around them, watch the people they knew grow old or vanish, and become something other than who they started as.

## The world

The post-apocalypse is populated. Mundane survivors — families, militias, cults, scavenger crews, refugee columns, warlords. Manifested humans, some worse than what baseline humans ever became. Ten metahuman species emerged from onset transformation, each clustered in specific regions, each more powerful at baseline than conventionally manifested humans, each breeding true. Eldritch entities exist — most are transformed humans, some small fraction behave in ways that cannot quite be explained by the human-sourced theory. Transformed fauna and altered flora populate the wilderness. Some are enemies. Some are neighbors, trading partners, unreliable allies, lovers, rivals. The landscape is a mesh of forces with their own goals, and the player is not the center of any of them.

Power blocs exist and remember. Relationships accumulate history across sessions. The world moves on its own clock whether or not the player is present, and the clock runs in years.

## Superpowers

Everyone who survived manifested. Powers are universal, varied, and unevenly distributed in strength — a ten-tier escalation runs from minor applied capability to genuine reality-bending, and most survivors live in the bottom three tiers. The highest tiers are the province of monsters, warlords, and people who are no longer quite people.

Powers fall into seven broad categories — physical and kinetic, perceptual and mental, matter and energy, biological and vital, temporal and spatial, eldritch and corruptive, and one additional category to be resolved in content — and a character typically has a primary affinity with secondary access. The interaction between categories, and between powers and the things being fought, is where tactical depth lives.

Powers are always on in the sim layer, not just in combat. A flyer sees the world differently than a walker. A regenerator needs less downtime. A mind-reader cannot have normal conversations. Powers shape how a person travels, how they shelter, how they scavenge, how they relate to people, who notices them, what kind of life is available to them. In combat they become tactical verbs; out of combat they are facts of existence.

Powers are not free. Every use costs the user something — physical toll, attention from things that notice, the slow corruption of using something poorly understood. A character who uses their powers constantly is burning themselves, burning bridges, or burning something worse. The fantasy is not *I am strong*. The fantasy is *I have this, and I have to decide when it is worth using*.

Powers grow through use within a tier ceiling. Most users gradually strengthen their abilities over years of practice. Tier advancement requires rare breakthrough conditions — specific crises, mentorships, exposures, catalysts — and breakthroughs always mark the advancer with specific costs.

Manifestation is circumstance-correlated with significant random component. What a person manifested reflects what they were doing, feeling, or experiencing at the moment of onset, modified by substantial randomness. Many people's powers are wrong for who they became. Individual relationships with powers are complex, sometimes traumatic, sometimes discovered late.

Powers are moderately hereditary — children of manifested parents manifest at elevated rates, with weak correlation to parental category and tier. Dynasties exist but are not deterministic.

## Character creation and goals

Character creation is session zero and it is play, not a form. The player discovers their powers, their origin, their entanglements, and their starting position through scenes. As part of creation, the player authors their character's backstory — who they were before the event, what they were doing when it happened, what they lost, what they still care about. From that backstory, a small number of personal goals emerge: narrative threads the character is pursuing, plus faction conditions that will produce recurring conflict in the way *Pirates!* uses nation loyalties to generate sword fights and ship battles.

Goals are not missions. They are pressures the character carries, and the game surfaces situations related to them as the player moves through the world. The faction conditions ensure that conflict finds the player without feeling imposed. The player can pursue their goals, ignore them, or outlive them. The world continues either way.

Beyond the authored goals, play is open-ended. The character survives, or tries to, and the shape of their life emerges from what they do.

## The core loop

Three loops stacked.

The moment-to-moment loop: the character is in a place, doing an activity or moving between places, and the situation presents choices. Read the situation, decide what to spend, commit, live with the consequence.

The session loop: pursue a goal or respond to a pressure over hours and days of in-world time, accumulating injury and resources and relationships and heat, and either reach stability or don't.

The life loop: across months and years and decades, become something. A character who lived. The game ends when the character dies, which it will, from combat or attrition or a bad narrative turn or all three at once — because the world is hard and one person is small and time is the longest pressure.

## What must be true of every fight

Every fight has a reason to exist beyond combat. The enemies want something, the environment matters, the stakes are legible before a blow is struck. No filler encounters. Every fight leaves residue — injury, heat, witnesses, resources burned, faction ledgers updated — and the residue is visible. Fights are short: a few rounds for most things, longer only when the situation earns it.

Disengagement, parley, and terms are first-class combat verbs. Killing is often the worst option. Most fights should be winnable but costly, and the cost should be felt immediately in the next scene. Lethality comes from accumulation across the character's life, not from the arithmetic of any single encounter.

Combat feels different depending on what the character is fighting. A rival survivor is a negotiation where politics is live throughout. A hive or a pack is ecological pressure where noise and blood draw more. Something from the dark places is usually a trap, and engaging it directly is usually the loss condition. The same chassis handles all of it; the register changes with the fiction.

## What must be true of the sim layer

The sim layer is not a menu between fights. It is the game. Travel has texture. Shelter is a decision. Sleep, food, and weather matter as pressures on the body and the day. Scavenging, crafting, treating wounds, hiding, training, and spending time with people are explicit activities with costs and outcomes. NPCs have names, histories, and memory — the important ones persist across years of in-world time and remember what happened. Factions act on their own clocks whether the player is watching or not, and their actions reshape the map over seasons and years.

The sim layer's quality is the quality of its state model. The narrator's job is to produce situated, grounded, textured prose drawn from the state — and the state has to be rich enough to give the narrator something specific to describe. Generic survival prose is the failure mode. The antidote is structured detail at every level: specific places, specific people, specific conditions, specific history.

## What must be true of character building

Characters are mechanically distinctive. A build should feel different from another build, not just numerically stronger or weaker. Power affinity and origin should produce combinations that play differently enough that the player wants to run a second character for reasons beyond *I died*. Progression is legible and long — the player can see the shape of what they're building into, and early choices matter for late options.

Progression runs on two tracks. Tactical progression — combat capability, power depth, tactical options — grows through use and training across years of in-world time. Life progression — skills, relationships, resources, reputation, territory, knowledge, accumulated memory — grows through living. Both matter. Both are expressed mechanically. A character who is a combat master but has no relationships and no resources is as vulnerable as a character with a network but no combat capability.

## What must be true of the engine

Two distinct subsystems sharing a character, communicating through a narrow interface.

The sim subsystem is a life simulator with map, time, NPCs, factions, activities, situations, and world state. Tick-based. Rich state model. Narrated in situated prose. The player's primary interaction with the game is through this layer.

The tactical subsystem is a combat engine: state machine, deterministic resolution, enemy AI, status effects, exposure tracking, powers as typed actions, injury and consequence. Activated when situations escalate. Returns outcomes to the sim layer.

State lives in code and files. Resolution is deterministic and enforced by functions. The model is a narrator over structured state, not an adjudicator of rules. Content is data — powers, enemies, places, NPCs, factions, situations — and adding content does not require changing the engine.

The context budget is a hard constraint. The model sees the current situation, the relevant state, and a short directive about tone and mode. Rules, lore, and history live in files and load only when needed. The narrator is Claude itself at runtime, reading payloads and producing prose directly, not making API calls.

## The simulation ground truth

The world's specific state derives from a Monte Carlo simulation that produced the "Lordly Equilibrium" worldline at T+1 year: population 15.94M in the mid-Atlantic region, roughly 278 polities, 51% infrastructure recovery, 18% corruption zones, stable multi-species political geometry with metahuman clusters coexisting with baseline human lordships, trade along the Chesapeake and Hudson corridors. The eight macro clocks track eldritch pressure, hive advancement, faction consolidation, infrastructure decay, corruption spread, metahuman integration, resource depletion, and generational succession.

The setting bible (factions, NPCs, locations, history, species, powers, eldritch taxonomy, narration register) is authoritative for all content. Specifications and code refer to the bible; they do not invent parallel content.

## Failure modes to actively avoid

A sim layer that is a menu between fights. A tactical layer that feels disconnected from the life being lived. A faction layer that only fires when the player visits. A Kenshi-lethal premise that the model quietly fudges. Exposition-dump character creation. NPCs that are lookup tables. Factions that are labels on territory. Goals that feel like quests. Time that passes without texture. Weather that doesn't matter. Relationships that don't develop. A decade of in-world time that feels the same as a week.

Combat that becomes "I attack" for fifteen rounds. Min-maxing that produces one dominant build. Heat inflation that collapses the political layer. The model's desire to be helpful overriding the world's indifference to the player's survival.

Above all: the failure mode where the system looks correct in the rules document and degrades under play because the model cannot hold rules, fiction, and state simultaneously. Every design decision is in service of preventing that.

## The one thing this game is about

**A life lived under pressure, with capabilities most people don't have and costs most people don't pay, in a world that does not care whether you survive.**

Every system, every mechanic, every piece of content serves that sentence. If a feature serves it, it belongs. If it doesn't, it doesn't. When two features conflict, the one closer to that core wins. This sentence is the tiebreaker.
