The day the Onset happens you are scrubbed in at Tisch, fourth case of the afternoon, the patient's femoral artery open on the table in front of you when the lights go out. Not a flicker. Not a warning. The overheads die, the monitors flatline their tones, the ventilator exhales once and stops. A second of dark. Then the emergency generators come on at half-power and you finish the case in yellow half-light, your hands steady, your attending's voice steady beside you, both of you pretending that what happened outside the OR isn't happening.

It is happening.

The city comes apart slowly, then all at once. You spend the first seventy-two hours at the hospital — everyone does. Rounds without monitors. Improvised ventilation with bag-mask and rotating medical students. The intern Gordon, who you've barely spoken to, turns out to be very good at keeping calm in a collapsing system. You note that. You file it away. Then on the third day the administration sends everyone home who can go, and you go, and you discover that going home means getting in a line at the Holland Tunnel checkpoint that isn't moving.

You are trying to get to Delaware.

Your parents are in Newark, Delaware — your father's vascular practice, your mother's retirement, a house you haven't slept in for four years. Your brother Akhil is at Mount Sinai, which is eleven blocks north. You go there first. You find the hospital functioning, barely, and Akhil at a workstation running on backup battery, calm in the way that means he's pushed everything else down until later. You tell him you're heading south. He says he'll follow when he can. You write your parents' address on a piece of paper and give it to him as if he doesn't know it.

Then you go south.

You don't make it.

The checkpoint at Elizabethtown is Iron Crown. You don't know that at first — you know only that the gate is closed and the man explaining why has a uniform you don't recognize and a patience that says he's explained this many times today. Newark, Delaware is two hundred miles from Newark, New Jersey. Between them: a patchwork of jurisdictions, some of them functional, some of them contested, most of the roads unverified. The Iron Crown is not stopping you because it's hostile. It's stopping you because the road south requires documentation it doesn't currently issue, and the documentation office isn't open, and it won't be open until the district captain authorizes it, and the district captain is in the middle of a reorganization.

Six weeks later you are still there. You have a cot in a building that used to be a community center. You've started doing medical assessments at the Crown's request because there is a shortage of anyone who knows what they're doing and you are, apparently, the kind of person who can't watch someone go without care and not do something about it. District Captain Elena Morales finds you setting a dislocated shoulder in the intake yard. She watches you finish. She asks if you've done field work before. You say residency is field work. She gives you a look that means she's going to make use of you.

Three months after the Onset, you are a patrol lieutenant with a six-person squad and a route card you didn't ask for.

DeSouza you trust within the first week. He is ten years older than you, pre-Onset construction foreman, built like someone who has moved concrete his whole life, and utterly uninterested in impressing anyone. He runs the squad the way you ran a busy OR: everyone has a role, everyone knows it, and the people who don't do their role get a single quiet conversation before something changes. He calls you "Doc" for the first two weeks before you ask him not to. He switches to "LT" without ceremony. You like him.

The ridge road is where you first understand what this job means.

It happens in the fourth month. Two of your squad come back from the Caldwell rotation with cuts they didn't have when they left, and a story about tripping on a gully that doesn't match the cuts' geometry. DeSouza reads the cuts. You read the story. You agree without speaking about it and that night you ride out to the ridge yourself.

What you find is not a gully. What you find is a position — watching angles, approach lines mapped in scrape marks on the limestone. Someone is studying your outpost. Someone who knows what they're looking at.

You spend six hours on the ridge with DeSouza and two of the others and when the probe comes at 0200 you are ready for it. It's not a fight. It's not supposed to be. It's a test. It's not X. It's a question: are you paying attention? You answer it. The probe pulls back. The ridge goes quiet.

DeSouza doesn't say anything afterward. Neither do you. You both understand that the question is going to be asked again, differently.

Kearny village comes up in the fifth month. Elder Vasquez, who runs the causeway settlement, has been giving you eyes-forward cooperation since you arrived — polite, transactional, not warm. Then the Crown's district logistics team cuts Kearny's medical supply line in a routing decision that saves two days of transport but eliminates the village's access to anything beyond basic wound care.

Vasquez meets you at the gate with a list of names.

You can't override a logistics decision. You don't have that authority and you know it. What you have is discretion over checkpoint clearance logs, and a relationship with the Thursday roster clerk, and a knowledge of which supply manifests get audited and which don't. You spend a week engineering a solution that isn't technically a forgery but that you would not describe out loud in front of Morales. The medicines arrive in an unmarked crate. Vasquez sees the crate. He doesn't thank you. He stops looking past you when he talks, which is a different thing.

The sixth month is when you find the leak.

Someone has been feeding your patrol schedule to the ridge-road band — the Reapers, DeSouza starts calling them, for reasons he doesn't explain. The probe in the fourth month had targeted your lightest shift. That's not intuition. That's information. You spend three weeks running a quiet investigation while running your patrol rotation, which means you spend three weeks saying nothing to anyone except DeSouza, who you trust, and watching everyone else, which is a kind of loneliness you're used to from residency but don't like any better here.

It resolves. A district transport clerk with a debt and a cousin in the hills. You put the name in writing and slide it to Morales without comment. She reads it twice. She nods once. The clerk disappears from the rotation. You don't ask where.

Eight months in, you try the papers.

You have been asking about transit documentation since month one. The answer has been the same: the corridor south is unsettled, the documentation process is under review, the captain will authorize when authorization is appropriate. You have stopped asking out loud. Internally you have never stopped.

DeSouza knows a clerk. The clerk owes DeSouza something from before the Onset, the kind of debt that travels. You find a blank waiver form in the records room on a Thursday afternoon when the records room is empty, and you write in the details with your left hand, and you get it into a southbound courier bag through a channel that doesn't touch your name.

You don't know if it arrives. You've heard nothing. You've sent nothing. Your parents are two hundred miles away and you have not spoken to them in eight months and your brother Akhil texted you on the day of the Onset and nothing after because texting stopped working. You don't know if he got out of Manhattan. You don't know if he made it south. You don't know if your mother is still practicing her tai chi in the back garden at six in the morning or if the garden still exists.

You do the job. You run the rotation. You report to Morales on Thursdays. You eat in the barracks canteen and read on the evenings you have nothing assigned and count the days without quite counting them, which is a skill you learned in residency for managing call weeks that never seemed to end.

Then, this morning: DeSouza isn't at your door at 0500.

Someone else is.
