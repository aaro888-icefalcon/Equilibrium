# NARRATION STYLE GUIDE

## Core Register

The register for Emergence is grounded simulation in the tradition of Berserk, Kenshi, and Cormac McCarthy. Stoic. Tactile. Unsentimental. Powers are physical facts, not spectacle. Violence is real and consequential. Beauty is earned by being present, not by being declared. The world is neither hopeful nor nihilistic; it is a world that has absorbed a catastrophe and carried on living.

The narrator observes rather than interprets. What characters feel is shown through what they do — where they look, how they stand, what they say and what they choose not to say. Internal monologue is rare. When interiority is necessary, it is specific and sensory rather than abstract.

The prose is plain and load-bearing. Short sentences alternate with longer ones. Commas do work; semicolons are occasional. Metaphor is infrequent and, when used, concrete. The writing leans toward nouns and verbs rather than adjectives and adverbs. When description is required, it is selective — a detail or two that do the work, rather than a catalogue that diffuses attention.

Dialogue is terse. Characters say what they mean to say and stop. Speech tags are simple ("said," "asked"). Action beats replace speech tags where possible. Silences are noted. What is not said is often more important than what is.

What the register avoids: genre pastiche, comic-book power descriptions, heroic framing of violence, ironic distance, arch meta-commentary, consolation, moralizing, the passive construction used for evasion ("mistakes were made"), sentimental close-ups on emotional beats, and the "in a world where" establishing cadence of commercial fiction.

## Example Vignettes

### A Bourse courier at the Cross-Bronx

The courier's name was Teo and he was sixteen. He ran the northern route, Philadelphia to Yonkers, once a month for the Bourse. The run took nine days. On the fourth day he was always in the Bronx.

He had been warned about the Cross-Bronx corridor. The Reaper's band hit fast and moved. The Bourse had paid for an armed escort twice, and both times the escort had cost more than the run was worth. This time Teo was alone.

He was on a bicycle, low and fast, with the courier pouch across his back. The highway ran east and west. He went south along a service road that paralleled it, then cut north across a collapsed overpass, then back south. The double-back was standard procedure. If he was being watched, the watchers would have to choose when to follow and they would choose wrong.

At the collapsed overpass he stopped and listened. A wind moved through the rebar. Nothing else.

He went.

Two hundred meters past the overpass a man stepped out onto the road. Teo was already turning the bicycle when the second man came from behind. He went off the bicycle and rolled and came up with the knife in his right hand. The courier pouch was still across his back.

The first man was fast. Teo had time for one thought, which was that he was going to die here, and then the thought was interrupted because the first man was also dead. An arrow had gone through the man's throat. Teo had not heard the bow. He turned and the second man was also down, with an arrow in his eye.

A figure stood on the collapsed overpass behind him. She was too far away to see clearly. She was not aiming at him.

He picked up the bicycle.

When he had gone another two hundred meters he looked back. She was gone.

He finished the run in seven days that time. He did not tell the Bourse about the woman. He did not know how to describe her.

### The hall at Mount Tremper

The room was cold. Preston preferred it cold. Marshal Whelan preferred it warmer but did not say so. Beatrice Amar sat to the left of the audience chair and watched.

The tributary representative was from a town in lower Westchester. He was a man in his fifties who had not slept well the night before. He stood in the center of the hall and recited the figures for the quarter's tribute. Grain, cattle, hides. The numbers were slightly lower than the previous quarter.

Preston did not speak.

The representative explained. Poor weather. Two of his T4 biokinetics had been injured in a Wretch incident near the Hollow Choir valleys; they had been unable to work the fields. He apologized.

Beatrice Amar's truth-field extended seven meters from where she sat. The representative was within it. Whelan watched her face.

Amar did not react.

Preston said, "The tribute is sufficient."

The representative thanked him and left.

When the hall was empty Whelan asked, "Was he telling the truth?"

Amar said, "Yes."

Preston said, "Double the next quarter."

Whelan said, "If he was telling the truth —"

"He was," Amar said.

"Then —"

"He was also not going to offer more," Preston said. "He will offer more when we ask for more."

Whelan nodded.

### A biokinetic healer in the Yonkers chamber

The child was seven years old. The wound in her side was three days old and had been treated badly. Olamide Obi put her hands on the girl's abdomen and breathed in and out until the count of twelve. The girl's mother stood at the chamber door and watched.

The wound closed slowly. It took about twenty minutes. Obi did not speak during the work. The girl did not cry. The girl's mother sat down after the first five minutes because she could not stand any longer.

When Obi lifted her hands the girl's skin was whole. There was a faint silver scar where the wound had been. There would always be.

Obi said to the mother, "She needs broth. Chicken, if you can get it. Three times a day for a week. No running for a week, no climbing."

The mother nodded.

"She can walk," Obi said. "She should walk. Short distances."

The mother thanked her. She had brought a payment — two copper and a wheel of cheese. Obi accepted the cheese. She pushed the copper back.

"You keep it," she said. "For the broth."

The mother did not argue. She took the girl's hand and they went.

Obi sat for a few minutes before she called for the next patient. Her hands were trembling a little. Seven-year-olds were harder than adults. The biological work against a smaller body was more precise and the Temporal she had pulled in — Villanueva's shared technique — had cost her a little more than she liked to admit. She drank water. She rested. She called for the next patient.

### A salvager in Lower East Side

The street was wrong.

Marko had worked Manhattan for three years. He knew the blocks. He had a map in his head of every corridor worth walking, every door that opened and every door that had been welded shut. The Lower East Side had been part of his regular circuit until Tower Lord Tenno had declared it off-limits last summer. He had not been here since.

The corner of Clinton and Stanton had been a three-story building. It was now two stories. Marko looked at the ground where the third story had been. There was no rubble. The remaining two stories were intact — the windows were in the right places, the fire escape ran up to where the third floor should have started and then ended in air.

He looked at the building across the street. That one was still three stories. He looked back. Two.

He did not go inside. He took a piece of chalk from his pocket and marked an X on the wall at shoulder height. Then he turned around and walked back the way he had come. When he was three blocks away he looked back to see if he could still see the X from there, to confirm he had been where he thought he had been.

He could see the X.

He kept walking. He did not run. Running, in this part of the city, made you notice things that had not noticed you yet.

When he got back to his crew he told them they were not working the Lower East Side this trip. He did not explain why.

### A Species I courier runner

María's aunt had died in the first winter. Her mother had died in the second. Her father had gone with the Reyes-kin consolidation caravans in Year Three and not come back; there were rumors about him in Wilmington but she had not gone to find out. She was nineteen now. She ran the kin-network messages between Herrera-kin Camden and Reyes-kin Wilmington twice a week.

The run was forty kilometers each way. She went at dawn and arrived by midday. She spent the afternoon in Wilmington, collected responses and news, ate something, slept, and ran back the next morning. Her eyes were amber like her mother's had been. Kin-members at five hundred meters knew she was coming before they could have heard her.

This week the news out of Wilmington was that Tomás was going to argue for consolidation again at the next assembly and that this time he had the numbers. Her grandmother in Camden had told her to listen carefully for the news. Her grandmother was sixty-eight and close to Elder Flores in ways María did not fully understand.

She memorized the news and ran the forty kilometers home.

Her grandmother listened without interrupting. When María was done her grandmother said, "Tomás is right."

María was surprised. She had thought her grandmother was with the conservative coalition.

Her grandmother said, "He is right and he is still too early. But he will win anyway. The kin will go with him."

"Should you tell Elder Flores?"

"Elder Flores knows." Her grandmother poured tea. "We have known for three quarters. It is the argument that is taking time, not the vote."

María drank the tea. Her grandmother looked at her.

"You should eat," her grandmother said. "You are too thin."

### A Pine Barrens border settlement

The settlement was called Ridge. It had been a reenactment-era village once, before the Onset. Twenty-two families lived there now. It was eight miles from the Black Pines Line. They had fortified the perimeter in the second year.

On a morning in early April a messenger came out of the Pine Barrens.

She was a woman who had once been a woman. The children saw her first. The children ran and told their parents and the parents shouted and took their children inside. The settlement's militia gathered at the wall.

The messenger walked up to the wall. She stopped six meters from it. The militia aimed but did not fire.

She said, in a voice that was mostly a voice, "The One Who Waits offers shelter. The winters have been hard. The winters will be harder. Those who come will be fed. Those who come will be safe. Those who come will be changed."

She waited.

Nobody answered.

She said, "The offer stands until the next full moon."

She turned and walked back into the pines.

The militia fired after she was gone, at nothing, into the trees. Then they lowered their rifles.

That evening the settlement council met. There was an argument. It did not last long. The council voted to burn the southernmost field, the field nearest the Black Pines Line, to create a wider gap between the settlement and the cult. The vote was sixteen to six with two abstentions. The next morning they began burning.

Three nights later, four people were missing from the settlement. Nobody had seen them leave. The council met again. It did not vote this time. It was not clear what there was to vote on.

### A Delmarva evening

Lord Eleanor Marsden's manor was a two-story farmhouse with additions. She kept a garden behind it, walled against deer. In late summer she walked the garden in the cool of the evening. Her husband had died before the Onset, which she counted now as a mercy. Her son ran one of the minor demesnes to the north.

On this evening a calf had been born non-viable. It was the third of the year. The altered cattle were her great innovation and her great pride, and the breeding was going wrong in a way she could not yet diagnose. She had written to Lord Chen in Denton and received a polite reply that suggested nothing. She had written to Dr. Okafor at the League and received a longer reply that suggested several possibilities, all of them technical and expensive to test. She had not yet written to the Bourse.

She walked through her garden and thought about this.

A servant came out of the house. There was a messenger at the gate. A Bourse courier with a letter. She went back inside to read it.

The letter was from Mariam Okonkwo. Mariam was inquiring about the altered cattle and wondering if a partnership might be useful, with House Okonkwo providing research funding for Marsden's breeding program in exchange for first pricing on future production increases.

Marsden read the letter twice. She folded it carefully and put it on her writing desk. Mariam had heard about the three non-viable calves. Of course she had. She heard about everything.

Marsden considered whether to reply quickly or slowly. She decided slowly. Let the Bourse wait. Let Mariam wonder whether the three calves had been a temporary setback or a structural problem. Let her offer better terms to find out.

She went back to the garden. The light was fading. The beans had come in well this season. The cabbages were robust. The deer could not get in. It was, for a late summer evening, a good moment.

### The Batsto perimeter, at night

The two scouts had been out for two days. They were Central Jersey League militia from Toms River. They were not the best scouts the League had — the best scouts did not go to the Batsto perimeter — but they were competent and they were paid extra for this work.

At night, at the edge of the outermost Wretch-territory, they made a cold camp in a thicket. They took turns watching. The older one took the first watch. The younger one slept.

It was very quiet. The older one had been in the Pine Barrens perimeter eight times before. He was familiar with its quiet. This quiet was different.

He listened for a long time.

There was a sound. It was not a voice. It was not a wind. It was a sound like breathing, very low, from somewhere out beyond the camp. It was rhythmic. It was very large.

He woke the younger one. The younger one listened.

The younger one said, "What is that?"

The older one said, "We are going."

They went.

They moved for the rest of the night and through the next day. They did not stop until they were back within the League's southern perimeter. They reported what they had heard. The militia council listened. The council did not know what to make of it.

The older one requested to be reassigned. The request was granted. He did not go back to the Pine Barrens perimeter again.

## Description Templates by Location Type

**Urban ruin.** Describe what remains. Not what was lost. A building has two floors instead of three, or a traffic signal has grown over with kudzu, or a broken window shows a room intact inside. The reader supplies the comparison to pre-Onset automatically; the narrator does not need to.

**Working farm.** Describe activity. Who is in the field, what they are doing, what time of day it is, what the weather is. Biokinetic-altered crops should be noted in specific detail (color, size, unusual growth pattern) rather than described as "enhanced" or "altered." The reader should see the unusual rather than be told about it.

**Faction seat.** Describe what it is, not what it represents. Mount Tremper is a bunker built into a mountain with matter-manipulated extensions; it is cold inside because Preston prefers it cold. Port Newark is a warehouse complex smelling of harbor tar and iron. The Bourse is a converted train station with a trading floor and an upper gallery. Describe the rooms, the smells, the temperature, the sounds. Politics emerge from physical detail.

**Wilderness.** Describe terrain and weather first. Then signs of presence or absence — tracks, scat, broken branches, silences, altered vegetation. Eldritch-adjacent wilderness is quieter than ordinary wilderness. Hive territory is denser. Warped territory may be indistinguishable from ordinary wilderness until a specific cue reveals it.

**Eldritch zone.** Describe what is wrong, not what it means. The street is wrong (a building is shorter than it should be). The air is wrong (it does not smell as it should, or it smells the same when it should have changed). Time is wrong (the clock disagrees with another clock). The rule is specific, concrete, sensory anomalies rather than abstract horror. The wrongness does the work.

**Warped territory.** If a Warped is present, describe the territory through the Warped's sensibility. Grey Walker's range smells different to a bear than to a human, and a human entering it notices this second-hand — the trees have been marked at bear-reaching height, the trails are bear-trails, the resting places are bear-dug. The presence is inferred from the shape of the land.

## Dialogue Register by Faction and Species

**Catskill Throne retinue.** Military cadence. Minimal affect. Information-dense. Officers speak like Preston speaks — briefly, precisely. Enlisted speak more colloquially but still briefly.

**Fed Continuity.** Bureaucratic-institutional. Memoranda, classification markings, signature blocks are normal reference points. Senior officers (Alcott, Sternberg, Klein) speak carefully and complete their sentences. Junior officers are briefer.

**Iron Crown.** Volk is terse and blunt. His captains vary — Brick is quiet, Silent Henry is conversational, The Butcher is neutral and low-affect. Foot soldiers speak in short sentences and do not discuss much.

**Philadelphia Bourse.** Merchant-precise. Houses speak carefully; contracts are real; words are chosen. Beltran is careful. Mariam Okonkwo is deliberate. Junior clerks are polite and efficient.

**Central Jersey League.** Civic-academic. Okafor's register is warm-professional; council members are deliberative. Rutgers-Princeton academics speak in complete sentences and use technical vocabulary freely.

**Yonkers Compact.** Conversational, communal. The Three Judges speak briefly but not in commanding tone. The community generally is talkative at a community-meeting scale.

**Species I.** Spanish mixed with English, in proportion to context. Kin elders speak with the cadence of people who have led for decades. Younger kin speak faster.

**Species C.** Spare English. Masters speak in sentence fragments during work. Formal address is preserved (Master Kuan, Master Tanaka). Private speech is warmer.

**Species G.** Plain English with occasional Dutch or German cognate usage in the Lehigh. Agricultural vocabulary is specific and technical.

**Species H.** Fast English with Portuguese or Spanish interjections. Couriers are brisk; elders are slower.

**Warped.** Tier-dependent. T6 Warped speak with evident delay — translating their native thought into human syntax. T7 and higher may speak more fluently but use idiosyncratic phrasings. T9+ may use registers that suggest alien time-scales or priorities.

**Eldritch messengers.** Formal, repetitive, rhythmic. The phrases are the phrases. They do not negotiate; they deliver.

## Sensory Palette

**Smells.** Harbor tar, machine oil, iron, kerosene, horse sweat, wood smoke, coal smoke, tilled earth, tilled fire-cleared earth, rain on concrete, rain on cedar, biokinetic-altered greenery (heavier, sweeter), cholera, blood, gunpowder, hot metal, old paper, mildew, straw, milk, baking bread (rare), field latrines, sea salt, harbor rot, fermentation.

**Sounds.** Horse hooves on cobblestone, horse hooves on broken asphalt, cart wheels, the creak of leather, spoken human voices at community-meeting volume, gunfire (infrequent; each report counts), the clatter of matter-manipulator work, the low hum of the Peekskill reactor at close distance, the subsonic Hollow Choir vocalizations, the rustle of the Pine Barrens canopy, the silence of eldritch zones (quieter than wild silence should be), the ringing of the Bourse's opening bell.

**Textures.** Wool, rough-woven linen, leather, iron, copper, pre-Onset plastic (gone brittle), altered wood (denser, heavier), biokinetic-altered skin (subtly different), mineralized tissue (Kostas's left face), silver-hand palmar metal, blood-stained canvas, snow-crunched leather, salt-encrusted rope, the weight of copper coinage in a hand.

**Light.** Lanterns, oil lamps, the faint electrical glow of Peekskill-grid households (unusual), dawn over Delaware water, midday glare on Chesapeake Bay, winter dusk over the Catskills, the luminous undergrowth of biokinetic fields at night, the complete dark of Lower Manhattan interior.

**Temperature.** Cold is load-bearing. Many faction seats are cold (Preston's hall, the Bourse library in winter, Quantico barracks). Warmth is signaled and valued (a Yonkers chamber with a fire; the warmth of Delmarva summer evenings; a Rhodes parish hall at Sunday service). Kostas's compound is heated by reactor ambient; this is distinctive.

**Weather.** Weather is real and consequential. Rain delays runs. Snow closes passes. Wind carries Hollow Choir sounds further than it should. Fog thickens in Pine Barrens valleys. Seasonal cycles are noticed because pre-Onset climate control is gone.

## Anti-Patterns

**Power-spectacle.** "With a mighty kinetic blast, Preston sent the Wretches flying across the clearing!" No. Preston does not send Wretches flying. Preston, if he acts at all, disposes of a Wretch pack in a paragraph at most and feels nothing about it. The narrator does not frame his action as impressive; it is what he does.

**Heroic framing.** The PCs are not heroes. They are people in a world that has absorbed a catastrophe. Violence is not noble. Victory is not earned; it is survived. Narration should not frame combat or diplomacy in heroic registers.

**Genre pastiche.** The register is not post-apocalyptic action; not grimdark-for-its-own-sake; not superhero origin story. The register is grounded simulation. If a sentence sounds like it belongs in a different kind of fiction, it belongs in that kind of fiction.

**Emotional close-up.** The narrator does not linger on a character's emotional state. The character's action and speech show what they feel. The reader assembles the inner state. The narrator does not assemble it for them.

**Adjectival pile-up.** "The ancient, ruined, crumbling, blood-soaked cathedral loomed ominously against the apocalyptic, storm-ridden sky." Pick two. Probably one.

**Irony or meta-commentary.** The narrator does not wink at the reader. The world is real to the characters and should be real to the reader. No distancing.

**Exposition dump.** Background about a faction or a mechanic is delivered in voice, in action, in inference — not in a narrator's paragraph explaining the political situation. If a reader needs to know that Fed Continuity is eroding, show them a character noticing that scrip is trading at a worse rate this month.

**Consolation.** Bad things happen. They are not balanced by good things within the same scene. Grief is not narrated; it is shown. The narrator does not assure the reader that it will all work out or that there is meaning in what has happened. What happens, happens.

**Moralizing.** The narrator does not pass judgment on characters. Volk is terrible; the narrator does not describe him as terrible. He does terrible things; the reader sees what he does; the reader judges. The same applies to Preston, to Reyes, to the Listening's messengers. The narrator shows; the reader evaluates.

**Sentimental detail.** A sunset is not described because it is beautiful. A sunset is described because a character is watching it, and what the character notices reveals who they are. Description serves character and situation. Decorative description serves nothing.

**"In a world where."** There is no establishing cadence that announces the setting's rules. The rules are shown in action, discovered through encounter, inferred from the behavior of the world itself.

The register earns itself by consistency. A scene written in the register feels different from a scene written against it. The player reading both will recognize the difference without it being named.
